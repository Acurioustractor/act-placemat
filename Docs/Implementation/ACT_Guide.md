* **Brand Essence & Values:** ACT’s brand essence emphasizes *innovation*, *empathy*, and *environmental sustainability*, leveraging **storytelling** and community engagement to address pressing social issues. The dashboard should echo these values by highlighting innovative projects, compassionate storytelling, and eco-conscious design elements. Every component (text, imagery, features) ought to reinforce ACT’s mission of social and environmental progress.

* **Target Audience Alignment:** ACT’s audience includes changemakers, collaborators, and supporters who “seek real stories of impact and transformation”. The dashboard content must resonate with these users – people who value collaboration, authenticity, and tangible impact. By focusing on human-centered stories and transparent impact data, the site will speak to visitors who share ACT’s ethos of collective action and innovation.

## Key Audience Needs & Dashboard Goals

The community dashboard will serve several stakeholder groups, each with specific interests. Understanding these will shape the content strategy:

* **Community Partners:** These are grassroots organizations and local leaders that ACT collaborates with on the ground. They will likely visit the site to see their work highlighted, find resources, or validate that ACT is honoring their partnership. The dashboard should make it clear that *community partners are at the heart of ACT’s work*. Content might include spotlights on community-led initiatives, testimonials from partners, or a section describing how ACT works *in solidarity with grassroots groups* to co-create solutions. This reassures partners (and informs the public) that ACT’s approach is bottom-up and respectful of local knowledge. The goal here is to demonstrate responsiveness to community needs and celebrate partner contributions.

* **Funders & Supporters:** Funders (like foundations, impact investors, or individual donors) and general supporters (the public who might donate or volunteer) will be looking for evidence that their contributions make a difference. For these audiences, **impact metrics and success stories** are crucial. The dashboard should present clear quantitative outcomes (e.g., number of projects completed, lives touched, environmental metrics achieved) alongside qualitative stories. This combination helps funders see both the scale of impact and the human stories behind the numbers. Funders are particularly interested in the *return on investment* of their support – not in financial terms, but social impact. Demonstrating how donations translate into real-world change (for example, “\$50 plants 100 trees in community gardens”) can be very compelling. By providing **concrete results**, the site will build confidence in ACT’s effectiveness, strengthening the case for continued or increased support. The content for this group should thus focus on transparency, results, and thank-yous to those who make the work possible (perhaps an honor roll of donors or interactive impact calculators).

* **General Public & Volunteers:** Members of the public who encounter ACT (via social media, word-of-mouth, etc.) might come to the site out of curiosity. For them, the dashboard should clearly convey *who ACT is, what problems it addresses, and how to get involved*. This includes having an engaging **storytelling narrative** that draws them in emotionally (for instance, a featured story of an individual or community positively impacted by ACT’s work) and **engagement calls-to-action** (like “Join our journey – subscribe to updates” or “Become a volunteer”). The tone here should be inclusive and inspiring, making them feel that *they too are part of the story*. In fact, using “you” language in storytelling (addressing the reader directly and showing how **they** can contribute) can help turn supporters into characters in ACT’s narrative, increasing their emotional investment.

* **ACT Team and Collaborators:** Although the primary audience is external, it’s worth noting that ACT staff and collaborators will also use this dashboard as a reference point. They might use it to direct potential partners or supporters to information, and they’ll want it to reflect their hard work accurately. An internal goal, then, is to build a tool that the team feels proud of and that is easy for them to update (via integration with their knowledge base or backend) as projects evolve.

By addressing the needs of these audiences, the dashboard’s overarching goals become clear: **educate** visitors about ACT’s mission and approach, **inspire** them through stories and data to trust and join ACT’s cause, and **activate** them to engage (whether by donating, partnering, volunteering, or simply sharing ACT’s story). With these goals in mind, we can design content and features that strike the right balance of emotional narrative and factual impact.

## Technical Implementation (React, Supabase, Notion, Slack)

With a clear vision of **what** the dashboard should contain, we turn to **how** to build it. A Curious Tractor’s tech stack will leverage modern, open-source-friendly tools: **React** for the front-end interface, **Supabase** for the backend services (database, authentication, storage), Notion as a content/knowledge repository, and potentially Slack for community or team communication integrations. Here’s how each plays a role in the implementation:

* **React Front-End:** React will be used to create a dynamic, responsive single-page application (SPA) or progressive web app for the dashboard. React’s component-based architecture is ideal for building out the sections described (stories, metrics, etc.) as reusable components. For example, we can create components like `<StoryCard>`, `<MetricCounter>`, `<PartnerLogoGrid>`, `<UpdateTimeline>` and so forth, which assemble into pages. Using React will also allow the site to be interactive (e.g., filtering stories by category, or hovering over a metric to see more detail) without full page reloads, leading to a smoother user experience. If SEO and initial load is a concern, we might consider using Next.js (a React framework) for server-side rendering of key pages (particularly for public content like stories). But initially, a standard React app is sufficient for an MVP. We will ensure the styling follows ACT’s brand (this might involve writing a custom CSS theme or using a component library that we style accordingly). Given the creative and slightly unconventional style of ACT, a custom design is likely – possibly using a utility-first CSS framework like Tailwind CSS for rapid styling, or styled-components for more custom feel. Accessibility should be baked in (contrasting colors, alt text for images especially those storytelling photos, keyboard navigation) to ensure the site is inclusive.

* **Supabase Backend:** Supabase provides an open-source backend-as-a-service (it’s essentially a managed PostgreSQL database with a JSON API and real-time subscriptions, plus auth and storage). We will use Supabase to store and manage content and user data:

  * *Content Storage:* All the dynamic content of the dashboard (stories, metrics, project updates, partner info, user submissions) can reside in Supabase tables. For instance, a `stories` table might have fields like `id`, `title`, `content`, `image_url`, `published_date`, etc. A `metrics` table might hold various metrics and their values, which the front-end can fetch and display. We can define `partners` table (with partner name, category, logo image, description) to power the partners section. Having content in a database (rather than hard-coded) makes it easy to update without redeploying code – the ACT team could use Supabase’s web UI or an admin tool to edit entries (like adding a new story or updating a metric value).
  * *User Authentication (if needed):* If we add features like volunteer sign-up or a community forum where users can log in, Supabase’s Auth can handle user accounts (email/password or social logins). In the MVP, the site is mostly public read-only content, so auth might only be used for an admin area where ACT team members log in to post new content or view submissions. Down the line, we could allow certain community members to create accounts to join a forum or comment on stories – Supabase can facilitate that securely.
  * *File Storage:* Supabase includes an object storage (powered by AWS S3 under the hood) which can store images and media. We might use this for storing photos for stories or partner logos. Alternatively, some images might be linked from other sources or the current Notion image links; but moving forward, storing them in Supabase gives more control. The React app can retrieve these images via public URLs or signed URLs as appropriate.
  * *Real-time Updates:* One neat feature – if ACT ever wants real-time updates (for instance, a live counter of something, or instantly reflecting a new story submission), Supabase’s real-time subscriptions could push updates to the React front-end. This might be used sparingly (e.g., a live donation counter or social media mentions ticker) but it’s good to know the capability exists without heavy setup.
  * *Integration with Notion:* While Supabase will be the main data store for the live site, we know ACT uses Notion heavily as an internal knowledge base. We have a few options to integrate the two:

    * **Manual/Automated Content Sync:** For example, ACT team could draft stories or updates in Notion pages (which they find convenient for collaboration), then when ready, those can be copied into the Supabase database (possibly via a simple admin interface or a script using Notion’s API to fetch content and insert into Supabase). This provides a workflow where the source-of-truth for content is still Notion during drafting, but the website pulls from a structured database for reliability and speed.
    * **Direct Notion Embeds/Feeds:** In some cases, we might embed a Notion page or widget directly (Notion has an API now, so one could fetch a page’s content). However, formatting differences and access control can be an issue, so for polished control we lean on Supabase as the published content store.
    * **Knowledge Base Links:** We can also simply link out to certain public Notion pages if they exist for deeper info (for example, if ACT maintains an in-depth documentation or resources list on Notion and makes it public via Super.site, the dashboard can direct users there for “deep dives” while keeping the main narratives on the site more concise).

  Supabase’s advantage is being open-source and aligning with ACT’s transparency and open ethos. If needed, ACT could self-host it or just enjoy that the data isn’t locked in a proprietary system. The use of Supabase also speeds up development significantly (as opposed to building a custom backend from scratch).

* **Notion as Supporting Knowledge Base:** As mentioned, Notion will continue to be a powerful tool for ACT internally. The notion workspace likely contains rich content like the **ACT Brand overview** and the detailed **Partnerships** philosophy we referenced, as well as project plans, research, etc. While most of that internal detail won’t directly appear on the public site, it will inform the content. The branding documentation ensures we adhere to correct messaging. Project databases in Notion could be used by the team to feed the project updates section (e.g., the team updates a Notion page for a project, and then periodically those updates are summarized on the website). In the longer term, if ACT wants a tighter integration, they could consider using the Notion API to pull certain data automatically. For instance, if there’s a Notion database of “Stories” or “Blog Posts,” a script could periodically sync it with the website. However, a simpler approach is often to maintain two parallel systems: Notion for drafting and internal discussion, and once finalized, content is pushed to Supabase for public consumption. This avoids accidental publishing of in-progress notes. Additionally, Notion can be used to house a content calendar (planning when stories or updates will be published) to ensure the website remains active post-launch. Using Notion as a knowledge base also helps onboard new team members or volunteers who might contribute to content – they can learn the voice and see previous content in the workspace. In summary, Notion supports the **content creation workflow** behind the scenes, while Supabase + React delivers the content to the public.

* **Slack Integration (Optional):** Slack was mentioned as a tool if needed. There are a couple of ways Slack could intersect with this project:

  * **Team Notifications:** The ACT team could set up Slack notifications for certain events on the site. For example, whenever a new person fills out the contact form or newsletter sign-up, an automated Slack message could alert the team (“New supporter subscribed!”). Supabase can trigger webhooks or use services like Zapier to connect form submissions to Slack, which can help the team respond quickly.
  * **Community Channel:** If ACT decides to run a Slack workspace for community engagement (inviting partners, volunteers, or interested members of the public), a link or invite can be embedded on the site. We should only do this if ACT is ready to manage such a channel (moderate it, keep it active). Alternatively, a *Slack-like* experience could be simulated via a chat widget on the site (but that typically requires more infrastructure). For now, a simple approach might be a **Slack invite button** that uses something like Slack’s invite API or a service to allow people to request access. Once in, those users could chat with ACT team or each other about projects, etc., building community in a grassroots way. This would underscore the *open and collaborative* personality of ACT, but it’s indeed a bit “rebellious” for a nonprofit to invite the public into their Slack – it shows transparency and approachability, breaking the mold of traditional orgs.

  If not Slack, ACT could consider down the line using a discourse forum or a Facebook group, but those are separate decisions. The main point is the dashboard should facilitate connections; Slack is one channel to do so. From a technical standpoint, Slack integration could be as lightweight as a hyperlink, or as involved as using Slack APIs to display certain content. One interesting idea: If ACT runs a Slack with an #updates channel, it could even embed the recent messages from that channel on the site as a pseudo-update feed. But that might be redundant with the project updates section.

In implementing the above, we should also keep in mind **performance and scalability**: React and Supabase can handle a decent load (Supabase scaling as a managed service, and the static content can be optimized), but we should optimize images (perhaps using a CDN or the built-in Supabase image optimization), use code-splitting so initial load is fast, and test on mobile devices since many users may access via phone. Accessibility and responsive design must be verified since community members and partners may have varying devices and needs.

**Security & Privacy**: We’ll ensure that any personal data collected (emails, etc.) is stored securely in Supabase (which provides row-level security and other features). If we showcase partner logos or photos of individuals, we must have permission for those (likely already granted if it’s in ACT’s materials, but worth double-checking). Following an open-source ethos, the source code for the site (likely held in the `Acurioustractor/act-placemat` repository) can be public, which encourages transparency and maybe external contributions. Since ACT values open-source, making the code available aligns with being *“authentic and transparent”* and could attract tech-savvy volunteers to improve the platform.

In summary, the tech stack and integration plan aim to provide a **robust yet flexible foundation** for the dashboard: React ensures a great user experience and customizability, Supabase covers data and scalability needs while aligning with open-source values, Notion supports the creative process for content, and Slack (if utilized) enhances community interaction. With this setup, ACT can manage and grow the dashboard over time without being locked into proprietary systems, and maintain full control over its narrative and data.

* **Storytelling and Copywriting Approach:** The content on the site will be written in a unified voice that aligns with ACT’s tone. This means every piece of text – from headlines to button labels – should undergo copywriting review. Key principles:

  * **Inspiring & Empowering Language:** We will use words that focus on positive change and empowerment. For example, instead of “Problems we tackle” we might say “Our Impact Journey” or “Seeds of Change We’re Planting”. The idea is to frame things constructively. When describing issues, balance the gravity with hope (as the brand tone says to balance issues with possibility). E.g., an impact story might acknowledge challenges but swiftly move to how the community and ACT together created a solution, leaving the reader feeling hopeful.
  * **Relatable and Conversational:** Write as if speaking to a friend or an equal collaborator. Avoid heavy jargon or academic language. For instance, use “we” and “you” pronouns to create intimacy (“Together, we can…”, “You’ll see how your support grows communities…”). The brand explicitly warns against being preachy or contrived, so the copy should feel genuine. If possible, include quotes or voices of community members in their own words – this not only adds authenticity but also breaks the monotony of one narrative voice.
  * **Highlight Mission & Values in Content:** We should weave ACT’s core values (empathy, innovation, resilience) into the narratives. For example, when writing a project update about an innovation in agriculture, mention how it exemplifies ACT’s innovative spirit. When sharing a story about a community overcoming adversity, mention empathy and resilience shown by partners. This reinforces brand messaging subtly.
  * **Call-to-Action Copy:** Buttons and callouts that invite engagement should be friendly yet action-oriented. E.g., instead of a bland “Submit” button, say “Join Us” or “Get Involved”. Instead of “Subscribe to Newsletter”, maybe “Stay Curious – Get Updates”. This ties the action back to the brand (curiosity, action).
  * **Review and Edit Process:** It’s advisable that once content is drafted (perhaps by a copywriter or team member), another person does a brand voice review comparing it to the brand guidelines, ensuring consistency. Over time, ACT can develop a short style-guide (if not already existing) for web content – covering things like preferred terminology (do we say “beneficiaries” or “community members”? likely the latter for a human touch), whether to use Oxford commas, etc. This ensures that as multiple people contribute content, the voice remains unified.

* **Brand Narrative Integration:** The site offers an opportunity to subtly integrate ACT’s unique brand narrative – the farm analogy and playful tone hinted in the “Our Brand” internal content. We might incorporate headings or section names that nod to this metaphor. For example, calling the stories section “Field Notes” or “Harvested Stories” (to play on the seed/harvest theme), or naming the newsletter “The Tractor Beam” (just a fun idea connecting curiosity and tractor, though this might be too whimsical – depends on how playful ACT wants to be). These creative touches can make the experience memorable and differentiate ACT’s communications from a typical NGO dashboard. However, any metaphor use should be clear and not confuse new visitors; it might be safer to use a subtitle to clarify (“Field Notes: Stories from the Ground”). Given ACT’s rebellious/whimsical streak, an element of playfulness is appropriate, but it should not overshadow clarity of information.

* **Accessibility & Inclusivity in Content:** Staying true to empathy, we should ensure the content is accessible to those with disabilities (proper alt text, ARIA labels, transcripts for any videos) and inclusive in language (e.g., using gender-neutral language where appropriate, avoiding idioms that might not translate across cultures if ACT has a global audience). The reading level of the copy should be considerate too – likely aiming for a broad public audience (so avoid extremely technical terms without explanation). This way, ACT’s message of inclusive change is reflected in the very way information is presented.

By rigorously applying the brand’s guidelines in design and writing, the finished dashboard will not only **look** like part of ACT’s family but also **feel** like it’s speaking with ACT’s voice. This consistency builds trust – a visitor subconsciously recognizes the professionalism and clarity of purpose. It also makes content creation easier in the long run: when a new story or page is added, creators have a clear framework to slot it into, ensuring it doesn’t go off-tone.

Before launch, we will do a thorough review of the entire site against a checklist of brand elements (colors, logo use, tone of copy, etc.), to catch any deviations. We might even involve a few trusted partners or supporters to navigate a preview of the site and give feedback on whether it “feels like ACT” to them.

## Next Steps & Roadmap

To turn this plan into reality, a phased roadmap will help manage the implementation. Below is a proposed sequence of next steps, ensuring we go from concept to a live, impactful dashboard:

**1. Define Content Strategy & Gather Materials:**

* *Inventory Existing Content:* Start by gathering all relevant content ACT already has. This includes success stories, testimonials, photos, metrics from reports, lists of partners, etc. Some of this may live in Notion pages, Google Docs, or past grant reports. Collate these into a content repository. For example, identify 3-5 strong impact stories that will be featured at launch, and ensure we have details and media for each.
* *Key Messages and Narrative:* Re-affirm the key messages we want the site to convey (e.g., ACT’s mission statement, vision, what makes ACT unique). These should be front and center on the site (perhaps an “About ACT” section or a tagline on the homepage). Draft a concise mission blurb if not already existing.
* *Content Outline:* Map the site structure in a content outline or sitemap. For instance: Home (with an overview and highlights of each section), About Us (mission, team maybe, though team info could be lighter on a public site depending on preference), Stories, Projects/Updates, Partners, Get Involved, Contact. Decide what pages and sections we’ll have at launch. The user prompt specifically calls for a **front-end dashboard** – this could mean a single-page style site with sections, or a multi-page site with navigation; a hybrid approach (anchor links for sections on a long scroll homepage plus detail sub-pages) might work well. Outline what content goes where and assign drafting responsibilities if multiple people will write.
* *Set Success Metrics for the Dashboard:* Ironically, as we build a site about impact, define how we will measure this site’s impact. Is success the number of newsletter sign-ups? Time spent on story pages? Contacts from potential partners? Having this in mind will inform some design choices (like we might prioritize an action if that’s a key metric).
* *Copywriting & Asset Creation:* Begin drafting the text for each section identified. Simultaneously, gather or create needed visuals (photographs, illustrations, partner logos). Where stories are concerned, get permissions for any personal anecdotes or images (ensuring ethical storytelling – those whose stories are told are comfortable with it). This phase will likely involve back-and-forth editing and approval, especially to align with brand voice.

**2. Design & Prototype:**

* *Wireframes:* Sketch out low-fidelity wireframes for the key pages/sections. This focuses on layout: e.g., where the hero image and mission statement go, how the story cards are arranged, how the metrics might be visualized. Given ACT’s creative side, we can be imaginative here, but wireframes ensure we cover usability (navigation menu, footer, etc.) before making it look pretty.
* *Visual Mockups:* Create high-fidelity mockups or a design prototype (using tools like Figma or Adobe XD) applying the actual branding (colors, fonts, images, content samples). This is where we see exactly how the dashboard will look and feel. Involve the ACT team and maybe a few external eyes for feedback. Check that the design indeed feels *grassroots yet professional, imaginative yet clear*. Iterate on the design based on feedback.
* *Design Review with Brand Goals:* Hold a brief review session comparing the mockups to the brand guidelines and the earlier content strategy. Ensure things like: Are we highlighting the mission properly? Is the partner section prominent enough? Does the site navigation make it easy to find key info for each audience group? It’s easier to adjust in the design phase than after coding.

**3. Technical Setup:**

* *Set Up Supabase:* Create the Supabase project (if not already). Define the database schema based on the content outline. For example, tables like `stories`, `projects`, `metrics`, `partners`, `subscribers`. Determine relationships if any (e.g., if stories are tagged by category or associated with certain projects). Apply Row Level Security policies as needed (for now, if the site only reads public content, we might make those tables openly readable via API for simplicity, or use an admin service key on the backend).
* *Supabase Storage:* Create buckets for images (e.g., a bucket for story images, partner logos). Upload any existing media or set up a process to upload through the app.
* *Supabase Auth (Admin):* Create service or admin accounts for ACT team to log in and manage content. Possibly use Supabase’s dashboard initially for direct DB edits (it has a spreadsheet-like interface). If needed, plan a minimalist admin UI built with something like Retool or even a simple React admin page that’s behind login.
* *Initialize React App:* Use a scaffold (Create React App, Next.js, or Vite) to start the project. Install Supabase client libraries. Set up the basic project structure and routing (if multi-page). Possibly incorporate a UI library or bring in global CSS with brand styles. Ensure the site is configured for deployment (maybe host on Vercel, Netlify, or Supabase’s hosting).
* *Environment Setup:* Store config like Supabase API URL and anon key in environment variables. Also, get any API keys for analytics (if adding Google Analytics or an open-source alternative like Plausible).

**4. Development of Features:**

* *Build Core Components:* Begin implementing the React components for each section (as identified: Story list and detail view, Metrics display, Updates feed, Partners section, Forms). Use dummy data or example state initially to get the UI correct.
* *Integrate Real Data:* Connect components to Supabase data. For instance, use `supabase.from('stories').select('*')` to fetch stories to display. Ensure data is rendering correctly. Adjust database or component as needed (often one realizes additional fields are needed, e.g., an `order` field for metrics to sort them, etc., and can still tweak at this stage).
* *Dynamic and Interactive Elements:* Add any interactive behavior – e.g., a carousel slider for stories if using one, animations on metric counters (there are libraries for odometer-like effects), form validation for contact form, etc. Ensure these interactions are smooth.
* *Responsive Design:* Test and tweak the layout for different screen sizes. Possibly implement a mobile menu for small screens. Check that images resize properly and text remains readable on mobile.
* *Accessibility Testing:* Use tools or browser inspectors to ensure the components have proper labels and roles. Do keyboard navigation tests (can you tab through links and forms logically?).
* *Slack/Webhooks:* If doing Slack notifications, set up a small function or use Supabase’s Edge Functions to send a Slack message on certain triggers (like new subscriber row inserted). Test it out.
* *Testing:* Do basic testing of the flows – submit the forms and see that data goes to the database (and Slack if configured), click through the site to ensure no broken links or errors, try odd cases like empty states (e.g., if no updates are available yet, what does that section show? Perhaps a message “More updates coming soon, stay tuned.”). Also test on multiple browsers (Chrome, Firefox, Safari).

**5. Content Migration & Review:**

* Once the development and content are both prepared, populate the actual content into the database. This may be done via SQL inserts, a script, or using Supabase’s interface. For images, upload them and ensure the links are correctly referenced.
* As content is loaded, do a round of QA on the live (or staging) site: read all text for typos or formatting issues, verify images are correct and appropriately captioned, check that metric numbers are accurate and up to date.
* Particularly, review the **storytelling content** with a fresh perspective to ensure it abides by ethical storytelling guidelines (no exploitative language, respect and dignity for subjects) and the numbers with a critical eye (are we reporting these impact metrics in a context that an outsider will understand? Do we need footnotes or links to reports for credibility? Sometimes a small “How we calculate this” link can be provided for the very curious data folks).
* If possible, do a soft launch or invite a small group of trusted partners/supporters to test the site and provide feedback (especially on whether navigation is intuitive and content is clear). For example, ask a partner if they easily found the section about their partnership, or ask a donor if the impact metrics answered what they’d want to know.

**6. Launch and Promotion:**

* When everything is ready and approved, deploy the site (e.g., to a custom domain like `act.community` or simply `act.place` if repurposing that domain). Ensure SEO basics are in place: good page titles, meta descriptions, social sharing metadata (so that when someone shares a story on Twitter, it shows a nice preview image and text).
* Announce the launch on ACT’s social media and possibly via an email to existing contacts. Encourage people to explore the new dashboard.
* Monitor the site in the initial days for any bugs or performance issues. Supabase provides logs; also monitor if any errors pop up in the browser console for users.
* Make sure the team is prepared to respond to any incoming queries from the contact forms or Slack promptly – early responsiveness will set the tone for community engagement.

**7. Ongoing Content Development & Iteration:**

* Post-launch, establish a rhythm for updating content. Perhaps an editorial calendar: e.g., publish a new story every month, update metrics quarterly or as new data comes in, add new partner info as partnerships form, etc. Since the site is a living reflection of ACT’s work, keeping it updated is crucial to remain relevant and engaging.
* Use analytics (web traffic data, link clicks, etc.) to see what sections are most viewed and where users drop off. For instance, if stories are very popular but the newsletter sign-up isn’t getting clicks, maybe the sign-up prompt needs to be more prominent or better phrased. Continuously refine the UX accordingly.
* Collect feedback from users. You could have a simple feedback form (“How did you find our site? Anything else you'd like to see?”) which can yield ideas for new features. For example, maybe supporters express interest in a specific type of content like interviews with team members or a forum to ask questions – these can be considered in future versions.
* Expand features if needed: down the line, ACT might integrate donation functionality directly into the dashboard (if not already handling via an external site), or interactive maps of project locations, or perhaps volunteer login areas with resources. The platform should be seen as scalable – we start with core storytelling and info, and layer more interactive capabilities as the community grows. Thanks to the chosen stack, adding new database tables and React components for new features is relatively straightforward.

By following this roadmap, ACT can move systematically from concept to a launched product that is both **comprehensive and true to its brand**. Each step ensures that we’re not just building a dashboard for the sake of it, but building a meaningful bridge between ACT and its community of supporters and partners.
