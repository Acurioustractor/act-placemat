## Overview

ACT Placemat is committed to respecting Indigenous cultures, protocols, and data sovereignty rights. This guide provides comprehensive information on cultural protocols, appropriate engagement practices, and the protection of Indigenous knowledge and data.

### Core Principles

**Indigenous Data Sovereignty**
- Indigenous peoples have inherent rights to control their data
- Data governance aligns with Indigenous values and worldviews
- Communities have the right to data ownership, control, access, and possession
- Data use must benefit Indigenous communities

**Cultural Respect**
- Recognition of diverse Indigenous cultures and protocols
- Respect for Traditional Knowledge and intellectual property
- Appropriate attribution and acknowledgment practices
- Protection of sacred and sensitive cultural information

**Community Self-Determination**
- Communities determine their own participation level
- Respect for community decision-making processes
- Support for community-led initiatives and governance
- Recognition of community autonomy and sovereignty

## Indigenous Data Sovereignty

### Understanding Data Sovereignty

Indigenous Data Sovereignty refers to the right of Indigenous nations to control data about their peoples, territories, and resources. This includes:

**Collective Data Rights**
- Data about Indigenous communities belongs to those communities
- Communities have the right to determine how their data is used
- Collective benefit must be prioritized over individual or commercial interests
- Traditional governance systems guide data practices

**Individual Rights Within Collective Framework**
- Personal data rights exist within community context
- Individual consent must align with community protocols
- Cultural advisors guide individual data decisions
- Community wellbeing takes precedence over individual preferences

### Data Governance Framework

**The CARE Principles**
- **Collective Benefit**: Data practices must benefit Indigenous communities
- **Authority to Control**: Indigenous communities have authority over their data
- **Responsibility**: Data users have responsibility to Indigenous communities
- **Ethics**: Data practices must align with Indigenous values and rights

**Implementation on ACT Placemat**
1. **Community Data Ownership**: Communities maintain ownership of their collective data
2. **Consent Protocols**: Culturally appropriate consent mechanisms
3. **Benefit Sharing**: Direct community benefit from data use
4. **Access Controls**: Community-controlled access to cultural data
5. **Review Processes**: Regular review of data practices with communities

### Data Types and Classification

**Public Data**
- General community information
- Public events and announcements
- Approved promotional content
- Non-sensitive cultural information

**Community Data**
- Community membership information
- Internal discussions and planning
- Community-specific resources
- Culturally sensitive but shareable content

**Restricted Data**
- Personal cultural information
- Community-specific protocols
- Sensitive historical information
- Information requiring Elder approval

**Sacred Data**
- Ceremony and ritual information
- Traditional Knowledge systems
- Gender-specific cultural information
- Information with spiritual significance

### Data Management Practices

**Collection Principles**
- Minimal data collection for specific purposes
- Clear explanation of data use and storage
- Community consent for collective data
- Regular review and purging of unnecessary data

**Storage and Security**
- Encrypted storage for all cultural data
- Community-controlled access permissions
- Regular security audits and assessments
- Backup and recovery procedures respecting sovereignty

**Sharing and Use**
- Community approval required for cultural data sharing
- Benefit sharing agreements for data use
- Transparent reporting on data use and outcomes
- Regular community consultation on data practices

## Cultural Content Guidelines

### Types of Cultural Content

**Traditional Knowledge**
- Stories, legends, and oral histories
- Traditional ecological knowledge
- Medicinal and healing practices
- Cultural practices and ceremonies

**Contemporary Cultural Expression**
- Modern Indigenous art and literature
- Community events and celebrations
- Cultural revitalization projects
- Innovation and adaptation stories

**Historical Content**
- Community histories and genealogies
- Historical events and experiences
- Documentation of cultural change
- Resistance and resilience stories

**Community Information**
- Community news and updates
- Resource and service information
- Community achievements and milestones
- Collaborative project outcomes

### Content Review Process

**Initial Assessment**
1. Content creator completes cultural sensitivity checklist
2. Platform AI system flags potentially sensitive content
3. Community moderators review flagged content
4. Cultural advisors assess cultural appropriateness
5. Elder review required for specific content types

**Cultural Advisory Review**
- Traditional Knowledge content always requires review
- Gender-specific content reviewed by appropriate advisors
- Sacred or ceremonial content requires Elder consultation
- Historical content may require community consultation

**Elder Council Consultation**
- Sacred or secret cultural information
- Significant historical events or traumas
- Traditional healing or ceremonial practices
- Decisions affecting multiple communities

**Community Consultation**
- Content affecting entire communities
- Historical events involving community trauma
- Cultural revitalization projects
- Policy changes affecting cultural practices

### Content Approval Levels

**Level 1: Standard Review**
- General cultural content
- Contemporary cultural expression
- Community news and events
- Public cultural celebrations

*Process: Community moderator review (24-48 hours)*

**Level 2: Cultural Advisory Review**
- Traditional Knowledge sharing
- Culturally sensitive historical content
- Inter-community cultural projects
- Cultural protocol guidance

*Process: Cultural advisor review (2-5 business days)*

**Level 3: Elder Consultation**
- Sacred or ceremonial information
- Traditional healing practices
- Gender-specific cultural content
- Significant historical events

*Process: Elder Council review (5-14 business days)*

**Level 4: Community Consensus**
- Major cultural initiatives
- Policy changes affecting communities
- Controversial historical content
- Cross-cultural collaboration projects

*Process: Community consultation period (2-8 weeks)*

## Community Protocols

### Traditional Owner Recognition

**Acknowledgment Practices**
- Acknowledge Traditional Owners at community events
- Include acknowledgments in cultural content
- Use appropriate language and terminology
- Recognize ongoing connection to Country

**Welcome to Country vs Acknowledgment**
- Welcome to Country: Performed by Traditional Owners only
- Acknowledgment of Country: Can be performed by anyone
- Different protocols for different ceremonies
- Respect for specific community preferences

**Language Guidelines**
- Use "Traditional Owners" or "Traditional Custodians"
- Acknowledge specific groups when known
- Use "Country" with capital C when referring to traditional lands
- Avoid generalizations about Indigenous cultures

### Community-Specific Protocols

**Engaging with Communities**
1. Research community protocols before engagement
2. Approach through appropriate cultural advisors
3. Respect community decision-making processes
4. Allow time for proper consultation
5. Accept community decisions gracefully

**Cultural Events and Ceremonies**
- Seek permission before sharing information
- Respect photography and recording restrictions
- Follow gender-specific participation guidelines
- Understand sacred vs public ceremony distinctions

**Traditional Knowledge Sharing**
- Verify authority to share information
- Seek appropriate permissions and approvals
- Provide proper attribution and acknowledgment
- Respect restrictions on further sharing

### Cross-Cultural Collaboration

**Best Practices**
- Start with relationship building
- Invest time in cultural learning
- Respect community priorities and timelines
- Ensure equitable benefit sharing
- Maintain long-term commitments

**Common Pitfalls to Avoid**
- Assuming universal Indigenous experiences
- Rushing consultation and decision processes
- Focusing on extraction rather than relationship
- Ignoring community governance systems
- Making commitments without authority

## Sacred Knowledge Protection

### Understanding Sacred Knowledge

**Types of Sacred Knowledge**
- Ceremonial practices and rituals
- Traditional healing and medicine
- Gender-specific cultural information
- Sacred sites and story places
- Seasonal and cyclical knowledge

**Protection Principles**
- Some knowledge is not meant to be shared publicly
- Gender restrictions apply to certain knowledge
- Age and initiation levels determine access
- Geographic and clan-specific knowledge systems
- Seasonal and ceremonial timing considerations

### Platform Protection Mechanisms

**Technical Safeguards**
- Multi-level access controls
- Community-managed permissions
- Elder approval workflows
- Automatic content flagging
- Secure storage and encryption

**Process Safeguards**
- Mandatory cultural review for sensitive content
- Elder consultation requirements
- Community veto powers
- Regular access audits
- Immediate removal capabilities

**Educational Safeguards**
- User education on cultural sensitivity
- Community guidelines and protocols
- Regular training for staff and moderators
- Cultural competency requirements
- Ongoing cultural awareness programs

### Sacred Site Protocols

**Location Information**
- Never share specific locations of sacred sites
- Use general geographic references only
- Respect photography and documentation restrictions
- Understand that some sites are gender-restricted
- Coordinate with Traditional Owner groups

**Story and Legend Sharing**
- Understand that some stories are location-specific
- Respect restrictions on story sharing
- Seek permission from appropriate knowledge holders
- Provide proper cultural context
- Avoid commercialization of sacred stories
